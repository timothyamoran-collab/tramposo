Organising Principle
Your touch does not need fixing — it needs automation. When receiving stops consuming conscious attention, that bandwidth is freed for what actually matters: the overlapping fullback, the CB who stepped up, the gap that just opened. Every session starts with the rebounder. Not as a warm-up — as the core habit that runs through the entire program.
Fundamental Habit · Practised Every Day
The Pre-Touch Routine
This is what separates the player who receives well from the player who receives well and already knows what they will do. The goal is for the sequence to be so automatic it happens without thinking.
1
Scan twice before the pass arrives. Not once. Twice. Left shoulder, right shoulder. Build the picture of the field before touching the ball.
2
Decide your first option before receiving. Not after. The decision arrives with the ball, not after the ball.
3
Your body shape already points toward that option. Body half-turned, hips open to the field. The reception loads the next movement.
4
Execute without hesitating. Doubt is the enemy. If you scanned well, trust what you saw. If the picture changes, your second option was already in your head.

Block A — Fundamentals
Bounce back · body shape · four turns
18min

Bounce Back — Dropping Pattern
4 min
This establishes the foundation of the dropping movement. Drop toward the rebounder (simulating checking away from the CB toward the midfielder). Receive half-turned — not squared up to the rebounder. One-touch back to the rebounder with good weight: a pass a midfielder could play forward first time, not a sloppy return. Reset, repeat. Simple but the body shape is everything — if you're squared up to the rebounder when you receive, the rep doesn't count.
Setup: Rebounder 4m away. Start 2-3m behind it, drop toward it, receive, bounce back. 45 sec work / 15 sec rest.
Half-turned on receptionWeight on the return passSquared up = rep doesn't count
Back-to-Goal — Four Turns
14 min
The four technical exits from a back-to-goal reception. You face the rebounder, receive with a defender cone behind you (between you and the target), and escape toward the target cone forward. Rotate through all four — spend 3-4 minutes on each, or focus on one or two per session if a particular turn needs more work.

(1) Drag back and turn — drag the ball back with the sole, pivot, accelerate to target.
(2) Fake one way, roll the other — shoulder drop one direction, roll the ball the opposite way with the outside of the foot.
(3) Outside hook — receive and hook the ball wide with the outside of the foot in one motion, body already turning.
(4) Spin — shoulder check first, sell a dummy one way, spin the other side of the defender.

After the turn — one amague before the target. Don't go straight to the target cone. The turn gets you the space; the amague beats the second defender. Keep it simple: pie a pie (small side-to-side feint at pace) or a shoulder drop. One move, then accelerate. This stops the drill becoming robotic and trains the full sequence — receive, turn, beat, release.
Setup: Rebounder in front. Cone 1-2m behind you (defender). Target cone 5m ahead (toward goal). Every turn exits toward the target — with one amague before arriving.

Turn and Finish — Four Exits
7 min
The direct continuation of Block A. Same back-to-goal setup — rebounder in front, defender cone behind — but now every turn ends with a finish on goal instead of just arriving at the target cone. The turn IS the move that creates the yard of space. The finish is what you do with it.

Rotate through the same four exits as Block A, spending roughly 90 seconds on each:
Drag back and turn — turn, one amague, finish.
Fake and roll — roll out, one amague, finish off the strong foot.
Outside hook — hook wide, one amague, finish in one flowing motion.
Spin — spin around defender, one amague, finish before they recover.

The amague is the same as Block A — pie a pie or shoulder drop, one move only. The sequence is: turn creates the space → amague beats the second defender → finish. Aim for a corner every time — not at the keeper, not vaguely at goal.
Setup: Same as Block A. Rebounder in front, defender cone 1-2m behind, goal or target 5-8m ahead. The turn exits toward the shooting position.

Angled Rebounder — Varied Heights and Angles
10 min
This is different from the floor rebounder work. Set the rebounder at different angles so the ball comes back at unpredictable heights — knee-high, chest-high, bouncing awkwardly, skimming the floor. Your job is to control it by whatever means necessary and bring it under your foot.

No fixed technique. Chest, thigh, instep, outside of foot, header if needed. The point is not to receive it cleanly — it's to never lose it. This is where the juggling skill and the real-match ball handling overlap.

Vary the angle of the rebounder every few minutes. Let it surprise you. This is the closest solo training gets to the bad passes you'll receive in a match.
Setup: Angled rebounder. Change the angle every 2-3 minutes. Start further away (5-6m), move closer (3m) for the trickier bounces. No target cones — just you and the ball.

10 min

Block B — Create Space, Place It

Four Amagues — 4 Minutes Each
20 min
Receive from the rebounder, execute the amague, feel the yard of space it creates, place it into a corner. Deliberate pace — slow enough to feel what the space gives you, fast enough to be honest. Pick a corner before you receive. Not after.

Fake shot — 4 min
Shape to shoot, let the defender commit to blocking it, shift onto the other foot, place it. The fake only works if it's convincing — really shape to shoot.

Fake to pass — 4 min
Shape to play it wide, defender opens up expecting the pass, shoot into the space they've just vacated. Natural for a second striker who teammates expect to lay it off — use that reputation.

Chop / cut back — 4 min
Receive moving in one direction, cut the ball sharply back across your body with the inside of the foot, shoot off the other foot. Good for creating the weak foot finish.

Stop and go — 4 min
Receive with momentum, kill the ball dead, defender overruns, shoot into the space behind them. Best when you've drawn the defender in with your movement.

Setup 2 — Triangle Receiving
Two cones + rebounder · the cone is a defender · use it
—
▼
Setup
Two cones and one rebounder forming a triangle. Rebounder at the top, one cone bottom-left, one cone bottom-right — roughly 4-5m between each. You collect near one cone, do something with the ball using that cone as a defender, then release off the rebounder and collect near the other cone. Back and forth.

The cone is not a gate to pass through. It's a defender to beat, use, or shield against. The touch before the release is where the work happens.
collect + release
cone = defender
return from rebounder
Variants
a. Outside to inside — collect outside the cone, one touch cutting inside past it, release from inside. The touch takes you away from the defender.

b. Inside to outside — collect inside the cone, one touch rolling outside, release from outside. The opposite direction.

c. Sole roll behind the cone — collect, drag the ball behind the cone with the sole, release from the other side. Slow and deliberate — feel the defender being taken out of the equation.

d. Shoulder drop — collect, drop the shoulder one way, shift the ball the other, release. Same as the match movement: the shoulder drop creates the half-yard, the release uses it.

e. Fake and go — collect, feign a pass one direction, release the other. The cone is the defender reading the fake.

f. One touch release — no touch before the release. Collect and release in one motion, using the angle of arrival to beat the cone without an extra touch. The hardest variant — the body shape on collection has to do all the work.
Setup 3 — Two Rebounders
Drop · recycle · receive · the full second striker sequence
—
▼
Setup
Two rebounders facing each other, you in the middle. Rebounder A behind you (5-6m) — the midfielder. Rebounder B in front of you (8-10m) — the ball played forward or in behind.

The base sequence: drop back toward A → receive half-turned → lay off back to A → spin and run toward B → receive from B in stride. This is the full second striker movement in one continuous flow: drop to link, recycle, run to arrive.

The timing is the whole exercise. The layoff to A and the run toward B have to happen as one motion — release and go simultaneously. If you pause between the layoff and the run, the moment is gone. In a match the CB has already recovered by then.

Variants
a. Base sequence — drop, receive half-turned, layoff, spin, receive from B. Get the timing fluid before adding anything.

b. Half-turn filter — receive from A already half-turned, and instead of laying off back to A, filter a pass directly to B in one touch. You become the link — drop, receive, filter forward immediately. No spin needed, just the quality of the first touch and the body shape.

c. Check and cut — instead of a straight drop toward A, check toward A then cut left or right before receiving. A's return ball finds you already moving sideways, half-turned. Then the layoff and spin sequence continues as normal. Face one rebounder for a set, then turn and face the other — each direction trains a different cutting side.

d. Vary the spin direction — in the base sequence, sometimes spin left after the layoff, sometimes right. The direction should feel like it's reading which side B's ball is coming to.

e. One-touch layoff — the recycle back to A must be one touch. First touch has to be already oriented for the layoff.

f. Receive from B and finish — the full sequence ends with a finish on goal after receiving from B. Drop → recycle → spin → receive → finish.

g. Third-man combination — the ball goes through both rebounders before finding you. Drop to A → layoff to A → A returns to you → you lay off to B → B plays you in behind → receive in stride. You're not making the final pass — you're arriving at the end of it. The timing gap between your layoff to B and B's return is the run. Go as soon as the ball leaves your foot.
Setup 4 — Release and Move
Rebounder + secondary target · don't stand still after the pass
—
▼
Setup
Rebounder 4m ahead. One target gate (two cones) 5-6m to the left or right. One target gate 5-6m forward. Receive from the rebounder, release to a gate — then immediately move to a second position and receive again. The point: every pass is followed by movement. You never stand and watch.

Variants
a. Release left, move forward — the basic sequence. Simple two-action combination.

b. Release forward, move wide — reverse the direction. Release through the forward gate, move wide to a cone, receive again.

c. Vary release + movement randomly — no fixed pattern. Decide during the receiving touch where you're going next.

d. One touch release — every release one touch. Forces the decision to happen before the ball arrives.

e. Weak foot release — any direction, weak foot only.

f. Add finish — after the move to the second position, instead of receiving again, receive and finish on goal. Sequence: receive → release → move → receive → finish.

Setup

You self-pass into space, run onto the ball, pass through the first gate, move immediately to the next gate, self-pass, receive again, pass through. The ball is always moving. You are always moving. Variants
a. Fixed route — always gate 1 → 2 → 3 → 4. Learn the footwork before adding decisions.

b. Random route — no fixed order. Decide the next gate as you're passing through the current one.

c. Weak foot through every gate — every pass through a gate is weak foot only.

d. One touch through gate — receive the self-pass, one touch through the gate. No extra touch to settle.

e. Add a turn — before passing through one gate per circuit, stop, do a back-to-goal turn (any of the four exits), then continue. Mixes the link play movement with the turning work.

f. Add a finish — the final gate each circuit leads to a finish on goal. Full sequence ends with a shot.
Setup 6 — Back-to-Goal Link Play
Receive under pressure · hold · release to different targets
—
▼
Setup
Rebounder in front of you, back fully to goal. Defender cone 1m behind you — tight. Two target cones: one to your left (left fullback), one to your right (right fullback). Optional third target: a gate 6-8m to one side simulating the channel ball for your striker partner.

The skill here is the first touch. Not shielding, not holding — the quality of where you put the ball with your first contact. Back-to-goal with a defender tight behind you, a heavy touch directly to your feet gets the ball taken. The right touch moves the ball just enough away from the defender to create the half-yard you need to release cleanly. Too little and they're in it. Too much and you've lost control.

The defender cone makes every touch honest. If the cone could reach the ball after your first touch, the rep doesn't count.

Variants
a. Touch left, release left — first touch moves the ball away from the defender to the left, layoff follows naturally. Get the direction of the touch and the release aligned first.

b. Touch right, release right — same, other side.

c. Touch away from defender, release opposite — first touch creates space to the left, but you release right. The touch is a fake — you move the ball one way, the defender shifts weight, you release the other. Hardest variant. Closest to a match situation.

d. Vary touch weight — same setup, but consciously experiment with how heavy the touch is. Heavy touch = more space but less control. Light touch = more control but defender stays in it. Find the range where you have both.

e. One-touch layoff — no touch before the release. Receive and lay off in one contact. The angle of arrival has to create the separation instead of the touch. Forces very deliberate body shape on reception.

f. Receive, turn, release — same setup but the first touch sets up a turn rather than a layoff. Touch away from the defender, spin, release forward. The defender cone makes every turn slower and more honest than Tuesday Block A.
