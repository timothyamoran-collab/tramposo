Organising Principle
The Tramposo is not defined by a position — it is defined by a mentality. Press. Hold. Turn. Finish. You are bigger, faster, and stronger than most in this category. That is a structural advantage you cannot train your way to. Every session exists to make that advantage dangerous — by adding the pressing angles, the hold up quality, and the finishing instinct that turn physical tools into goals.
Fundamental Habit · Practised Every Day
The Pre-Touch Routine
This is what separates the player who receives well from the player who receives well and already knows what they will do. The goal is for the sequence to be so automatic it happens without thinking.
1
Scan twice before the pass arrives. Not once. Twice. Left shoulder, right shoulder. Build the picture of the field before touching the ball.
2
Decide your first option before receiving. Not after. The decision arrives with the ball, not after the ball.
3
Your body shape already points toward that option. Body half-turned, hips open to the field. The reception loads the next movement.
4
Execute without hesitating. Doubt is the enemy. If you scanned well, trust what you saw. If the picture changes, your second option was already in your head.

Heading — Contact, Timing & Aim
Self-throw · forehead · eyes open · attack not wait
8min
▼
Contact — Getting It Right First
3 min
Before you worry about power or direction, the contact point has to be right. Most bad headers happen because of two things: heading with the top of the skull instead of the forehead, and closing the eyes at the moment of impact.

Contact point: The centre of the flat part of your forehead — roughly between the hairline and the eyebrows. This is the hardest, most stable surface. Headers off the top of the skull are weak and unpredictable. Headers off the side of the forehead deflect unpredictably.

Eyes open: Keep your eyes on the ball through contact, not just until contact. The instinct is to flinch and close them just before impact. Fight that instinct — if your eyes are open you can adjust at the last moment and your neck muscles stay engaged.

Neck muscles: The power in a header doesn't come from the ball hitting your head — it comes from you driving your head through the ball. Tense the neck, lead with the forehead, drive forward into contact.

Throw the ball gently, head it at a wall target, and focus entirely on these three things. Not power. Contact quality only.
Flat forehead — not top of skullEyes open through contactDrive through — don't receive
Timing — Attacking at the Top
2 min
Timing is the most important aerial skill and the hardest to develop. The instinct is to wait for the ball to drop to a comfortable height — but that's the defender's timeline, not yours. Attacking the ball at the top of its arc means you get there first.

The drill: Throw the ball higher than before. Take one step forward and attack it before it reaches its natural dropping point. The contact happens while the ball is still rising or at its peak — not on its way down.

What it feels like at first: Wrong. You'll feel like you're jumping too early. That's correct — you're retraining the instinct. Over time arriving early starts to feel natural and waiting starts to feel late.

Count your steps from the throw to the contact. You want to be attacking the ball on step 2 or 3 — not step 5 or 6 after it's dropped.
Attack at the top — not on the way downOne step forward into itEarly feels wrong at first — correct
Aim — Redirecting with the Forehead
3 min
Once contact and timing are grooved, aim is about turning your head and neck to redirect the ball rather than just driving it straight. This is the far post redirect — the ball comes from one side, you meet it and send it across to the opposite corner.

The drill: Throw the ball slightly to your right. Attack it with the forehead but turn your head left through contact — the ball should redirect to the left wall target. Then reverse: throw left, redirect right.

The key: The turn happens through the contact, not after it. If you turn too early you lose the contact point. If you turn too late the ball goes straight. The turn and the contact are simultaneous.

This simulates the most common headed finish you'll get as a 9: arriving at the far post and redirecting a cross across the keeper into the opposite corner. The power is irrelevant — the redirection is everything.

Receive, Shield, Release
8 min
The foundation of hold up play. Receive back to goal with a defender cone tight behind you. The job is not to turn — it's to shield the ball and release cleanly to a target. The touch must move the ball just enough away from the cone to create the half-yard for the release. Too heavy and you lose control. Too light and the cone gets it.

Two release targets: one to your left (left fullback), one to your right (right fullback). Decide which target before the ball arrives — scan, commit, execute. The defender cone makes every touch honest. If the cone could reach the ball after your first touch, the rep doesn't count.
Setup: Rebounder 4m in front. Defender cone 1m behind you — tight. Two target gates 4m to each side. 45 sec work / 15 sec rest.
Body between ball and coneDecide before receivingCone reaches ball = rep doesn't count
Receive, Shield, Turn
12 min
Same setup as above but now the job is to turn rather than release. The defender cone represents a CB who is going to press you — you receive, shield to buy a half-second, then execute one of the four exits toward the forward target. This is the moment hold up play becomes a threat.

(1) Drag back and turn — drag with the sole, pivot, accelerate.
(2) Fake and roll — shoulder drop one way, roll opposite with outside of foot.
(3) Outside hook — hook wide in one motion, body already turning.
(4) Spin — shoulder check, sell dummy, spin the other side.

After the turn — one amague before the target. The sequence is: shield → turn → amague → accelerate. Spend 3 minutes on each exit or focus on the ones that need work.
Setup: Rebounder in front. Defender cone 1m behind. Forward target cone 5m ahead toward goal. 45 sec work / 15 sec rest.

Block A — Pressing Mechanics

Curved Pressing Sprint
10 min
The foundational pressing pattern. A straight sprint at the CB gives them an easy escape — they know exactly where you're going and can play around you. The curved run cuts off the pass to the fullback before you arrive, so the CB has fewer options the closer you get. That's what forces the long ball.

The curve is the skill. It should feel unnatural at first — your instinct is to go straight at the ball. Fight that instinct. The curve bends wide toward the fullback cone, then arrives at the CB cone from an angle that blocks the pass lane. Body sideways on arrival, last two steps controlled — you want to be able to change direction, not run past them.

Weeks 1-3: 6 reps, full recovery between each. Groove the shape.
Weeks 4-6: 8 reps. Same quality.
Phase 2+: Two sets of 6, 90 seconds between sets.
Setup: CB cone 12-15m ahead (further than the warehouse — use the pitch). FB cone 5m to one side. Your curved run passes in front of the FB cone before arriving at the CB cone from an angle. Jog back to start between reps.

Trigger Drill — Stalk and Explode
5 min
The curved sprint alone is just fitness. The trigger is what makes it pressing. Walk slowly toward the CB cone — stalking, not sprinting. This simulates the positioning phase, the moment you're waiting for. At a self-decided trigger moment — imagine the CB takes a heavy touch, or plays it back to the keeper — explode into the curved sprint.

The trigger must be a specific imagined moment, not just whenever you feel like going. The walk forces you to be patient. The explosion is total — zero to full speed in two steps. This is the quality that makes pressing dangerous: the CB doesn't know when it's coming.

Variation across weeks: Some weeks, trigger off a heavy touch. Other weeks, trigger off a back pass. Keep alternating so the pattern stays honest.
Setup: Same cone layout as curved sprint. 5-6 reps. Full recovery.
Patient walk firstTrigger must be specificZero to full speed — no half-press

Steep Rebounder — Dropping Ball Control
8 min
Angle the rebounder steeply so the ball comes back high and dropping rather than flat. This is the long ball from your goalkeeper or a cleared defensive header — not a crisp pass but a ball falling from height with a defender arriving behind you.

The first touch is the skill. It needs to land somewhere useful — not back at the rebounder, not behind you where the defender cone is. Practice with chest, thigh, and feet. The chest touch is the primary surface. Defender cone 1m behind you on every rep — if the cone could get the ball after your first touch, the rep doesn't count.
Setup: Rebounder angled steep, 3-4m distance. Defender cone 1m behind. Two release gates to each side. 45 sec work / 15 sec rest.
Chest is primary surfaceTouch away from coneAttack before it drops — don't wait
Self-Throw Reception — Three Trajectories
8 min
Throw the ball up yourself and vary the trajectory. This lets you practice scenarios the rebounder can't replicate.

High ball straight down — throw directly above, receive with chest or thigh, control away from defender cone, release to a gate.

Ball dropping in front — throw slightly forward, take a step and attack it at pace. This is the ball played in behind for you to run onto. First touch must keep you moving forward.

Attack at height — throw higher, attack the ball before it reaches its natural dropping point. The habit that wins aerial contests — going through it before the defender arrives.

Spend roughly 2-3 minutes per trajectory.
Setup: Open space on the pitch. Defender cone 1m behind for the first trajectory. Goal or wall target for finishing.
Vary trajectory every repAttack — don't waitFirst touch forward on pace balls
Wall Rebound — Receive at Pace
5 min
Kick the ball hard at a wall at an angle — not straight at it, at an angle so the rebound comes back across your body. Run to meet the rebound before it settles. Control it and finish on goal.

The rebound is unpredictable in a way the rebounder and self-throws aren't. The pace, the bounce, the angle — all vary. You have to read it while moving, which is the closest solo equivalent to receiving under pressure from a teammate's clearance or a deflected ball in the midfield.

Vary the kick angle across reps — shallow angle gives you a rebound that runs across you, steep angle gives you one that comes back fast and direct. Both are game situations.

First touch must keep you moving forward. A touch that kills the ball dead is a failed rep — in a game that's when the defender catches you. The control and the next action are one continuous movement.
Setup: Wall with open space. Start 5-6m from the wall. Vary kick angle each rep. Goal or target behind you to finish toward.
Read the rebound while movingFirst touch forward — never deadUnpredictable is the pointFinish every rep

Angled Rebounder — Varied Heights and Angles
10 min
This is different from the floor rebounder work. Set the rebounder at different angles so the ball comes back at unpredictable heights — knee-high, chest-high, bouncing awkwardly, skimming the floor. Your job is to control it by whatever means necessary and bring it under your foot.

No fixed technique. Chest, thigh, instep, outside of foot, header if needed. The point is not to receive it cleanly — it's to never lose it. This is where the juggling skill and the real-match ball handling overlap.

Vary the angle of the rebounder every few minutes. Let it surprise you. This is the closest solo training gets to the bad passes you'll receive in a match.
Setup: Angled rebounder. Change the angle every 2-3 minutes. Start further away (5-6m), move closer (3m) for the trickier bounces. No target cones — just you and the ball.

Five Amagues — Rotating
8 min
Receive from the rebounder, create space with one move, finish. Pick a corner before you receive. Roughly 90 seconds on each move:

Shoulder drop — drop the shoulder hard one way, shift the ball the other, the defender commits and you're already gone. This is the one that feels natural — use it. The shoulder drop is deception in service of power, not flair.

Fake shot — shape to shoot, shift foot, place it.
Fake to pass — shape to lay off, shoot into the vacated space.
Chop / cut back — inside of foot across the body, shoot off the other foot.
Stop and go — kill the ball dead, imaginary defender overruns, shoot.

The amague creates the yard. The finish uses it. One move only — don't chain them.

No ball. Cones for the CB positions. Walk each of the five runs slowly, then at pace. One rep each. The goal is to make the runs automatic so they don't require conscious thought in a game.

Run 1 — Central, central MF receives: Fake step toward one CB, diagonal in behind the other.

Run 2 — Central, near-side FB receives: Both CBs compress toward ball. Run away from ball, diagonal in behind far-side CB.

Run 3 — Central, far-side FB receives: Mirror of Run 2.

Run 4 — Half-space, near-side FB or central MF receives: Read the CB. If they track — cut inside. If they ball-watch — run in behind.

Run 5 — Half-space, far-side FB receives: CBs shift away from you. Accelerate diagonally inside and in behind into the vacated channel.

Pressing Variations
Run the Wednesday curved pressing sprint with variations not covered in the main session.

Double press: Press the CB cone, they play it to the FB cone, immediately recover and press the FB cone from a new angle. Two consecutive curved sprints — the second one harder because you're already tired. This is the most match-realistic pressing drill in the whole program.

Press from wider starting position: Start 5m wider than usual — simulating pressing as the ball-side striker rather than the central one. The curve is sharper, the angle is different, the pass lane you're cutting is different.

Press and win: After arriving at the CB cone, the ball comes back at you — a short pass from the cone position. Control it immediately and finish. Simulates winning the ball in the press and having one touch to do something with it.

Extra finishing reps from positions and angles not covered in the weekly sessions.

Setup 2 — Triangle Receiving
Two cones + rebounder · the cone is a defender · use it
—
▼
Setup
Two cones and one rebounder forming a triangle. Rebounder at the top, one cone bottom-left, one cone bottom-right — roughly 4-5m between each. You collect near one cone, do something with the ball using that cone as a defender, then release off the rebounder and collect near the other cone. Back and forth.

The cone is not a gate to pass through. It's a defender to beat, use, or shield against. The touch before the release is where the work happens.
collect + release
cone = defender
return from rebounder
Variants
a. Outside to inside — collect outside the cone, one touch cutting inside past it, release from inside. The touch takes you away from the defender.

b. Inside to outside — collect inside the cone, one touch rolling outside, release from outside. The opposite direction.

c. Sole roll behind the cone — collect, drag the ball behind the cone with the sole, release from the other side. Slow and deliberate — feel the defender being taken out of the equation.

d. Shoulder drop — collect, drop the shoulder one way, shift the ball the other, release. Same as the match movement: the shoulder drop creates the half-yard, the release uses it.

e. Fake and go — collect, feign a pass one direction, release the other. The cone is the defender reading the fake.

f. One touch release — no touch before the release. Collect and release in one motion, using the angle of arrival to beat the cone without an extra touch. The hardest variant — the body shape on collection has to do all the work.
Setup 3 — Two Rebounders
Drop · recycle · receive · the full second striker sequence
—
▼
Setup
Two rebounders facing each other, you in the middle. Rebounder A behind you (5-6m) — the midfielder. Rebounder B in front of you (8-10m) — the ball played forward or in behind.

The base sequence: drop back toward A → receive half-turned → lay off back to A → spin and run toward B → receive from B in stride. This is the full second striker movement in one continuous flow: drop to link, recycle, run to arrive.

The timing is the whole exercise. The layoff to A and the run toward B have to happen as one motion — release and go simultaneously. If you pause between the layoff and the run, the moment is gone. In a match the CB has already recovered by then.
① drop to receive
③ layoff · ④ spin and run
⑤ receive in stride
Variants
a. Base sequence — drop, receive half-turned, layoff, spin, receive from B. Get the timing fluid before adding anything.

b. Half-turn filter — receive from A already half-turned, and instead of laying off back to A, filter a pass directly to B in one touch. You become the link — drop, receive, filter forward immediately. No spin needed, just the quality of the first touch and the body shape.

c. Check and cut — instead of a straight drop toward A, check toward A then cut left or right before receiving. A's return ball finds you already moving sideways, half-turned. Then the layoff and spin sequence continues as normal. Face one rebounder for a set, then turn and face the other — each direction trains a different cutting side.

d. Vary the spin direction — in the base sequence, sometimes spin left after the layoff, sometimes right. The direction should feel like it's reading which side B's ball is coming to.

e. One-touch layoff — the recycle back to A must be one touch. First touch has to be already oriented for the layoff.

f. Receive from B and finish — the full sequence ends with a finish on goal after receiving from B. Drop → recycle → spin → receive → finish.

g. Third-man combination — the ball goes through both rebounders before finding you. Drop to A → layoff to A → A returns to you → you lay off to B → B plays you in behind → receive in stride. You're not making the final pass — you're arriving at the end of it. The timing gap between your layoff to B and B's return is the run. Go as soon as the ball leaves your foot.
Setup 4 — Release and Move
Rebounder + secondary target · don't stand still after the pass
—
▼
Setup
Rebounder 4m ahead. One target gate (two cones) 5-6m to the left or right. One target gate 5-6m forward. Receive from the rebounder, release to a gate — then immediately move to a second position and receive again. The point: every pass is followed by movement. You never stand and watch.

Variants
a. Release left, move forward — the basic sequence. Simple two-action combination.

b. Release forward, move wide — reverse the direction. Release through the forward gate, move wide to a cone, receive again.

c. Vary release + movement randomly — no fixed pattern. Decide during the receiving touch where you're going next.

d. One touch release — every release one touch. Forces the decision to happen before the ball arrives.

e. Weak foot release — any direction, weak foot only.

f. Add finish — after the move to the second position, instead of receiving again, receive and finish on goal. Sequence: receive → release → move → receive → finish.

Setup
No rebounder. Three or four cone gates spread across a 15×15m area — each gate is two cones 1.5m apart. Variants
a. Fixed route — always gate 1 → 2 → 3 → 4. Learn the footwork before adding decisions.

b. Random route — no fixed order. Decide the next gate as you're passing through the current one.

Setup 6 — Back-to-Goal Link Play
Receive under pressure · hold · release to different targets
—
▼
Setup
Rebounder in front of you, back fully to goal. Defender cone 1m behind you — tight. Two target cones: one to your left (left fullback), one to your right (right fullback). Optional third target: a gate 6-8m to one side simulating the channel ball for your striker partner.

The skill here is the first touch. Not shielding, not holding — the quality of where you put the ball with your first contact. Back-to-goal with a defender tight behind you, a heavy touch directly to your feet gets the ball taken. The right touch moves the ball just enough away from the defender to create the half-yard you need to release cleanly. Too little and they're in it. Too much and you've lost control.

The defender cone makes every touch honest. If the cone could reach the ball after your first touch, the rep doesn't count.
ball from midfielder
defender tight behind
release targets
Variants
a. Touch left, release left — first touch moves the ball away from the defender to the left, layoff follows naturally. Get the direction of the touch and the release aligned first.

b. Touch right, release right — same, other side.

c. Touch away from defender, release opposite — first touch creates space to the left, but you release right. The touch is a fake — you move the ball one way, the defender shifts weight, you release the other. Hardest variant. Closest to a match situation.

d. Vary touch weight — same setup, but consciously experiment with how heavy the touch is. Heavy touch = more space but less control. Light touch = more control but defender stays in it. Find the range where you have both.

e. One-touch layoff — no touch before the release. Receive and lay off in one contact. The angle of arrival has to create the separation instead of the touch. Forces very deliberate body shape on reception.

f. Receive, turn, release — same setup but the first touch sets up a turn rather than a layoff. Touch away from the defender, spin, release forward. The defender cone makes every turn slower and more honest than Tuesday Block A.
Self-Assessment · Every Two Weeks · After a Competitive Game
The Five Questions
1
Am I pressing with a real trigger — a specific moment I've decided to go — or am I just running hard in the general direction of the ball?
2
When the long ball arrives, am I attacking it or waiting for it? Is my first touch killing the situation or keeping it alive?
3
Am I arriving into dangerous positions — or am I watching dangerous positions develop from somewhere safe?
4
Does hunting mode arrive on its own from the first minute, or do I still have to consciously switch it on?
5
Is there one moment from this game — a press, a run, a finish — that felt automatic? And one that still felt rehearsed?
